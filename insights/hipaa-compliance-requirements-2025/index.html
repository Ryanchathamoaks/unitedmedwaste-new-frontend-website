HIPAA Compliance Requirements 2026: Waste & PHI Guide

HIPAA compliance requirements for 2026 covered entities: PHI disposal, BAAs, breach notification, Security Rule updates, and waste handling for healthcare.

Back to Insights

HIPAA compliance requirements are the federal standards that healthcare facilities, health plans, clearinghouses, and their vendors must follow to protect patient information. The rules apply to electronic, paper, and physical forms of protected health information (PHI), including the records, labels, prescription bottles, wristbands, and specimen containers that move through a facility’s waste stream every day.

  • Compliance is built on four rules: the Privacy Rule, the Security Rule, the Breach Notification Rule, and the Enforcement Rule.

Most healthcare leaders read about HIPAA compliance and picture firewalls, encrypted laptops, and EHR audit logs. Those matter. But the most enforced and most expensive HIPAA failures often happen at the loading dock, the shred bin, the pharmacy counter, and the back hallway where waste is staged for pickup.

The 2025 regulatory landscape, the proposed Security Rule overhaul, and a new wave of Office for Civil Rights enforcement have shifted what facilities need to do to stay compliant.

What Are HIPAA Compliance Requirements?

HIPAA compliance requirements obligate covered entities and their business associates to protect PHI through written policies, workforce training, access controls, secure disposal procedures, signed Business Associate Agreements, breach response procedures, and administrative, physical, and technical safeguards for electronic PHI.

When reviewing staff training, recordkeeping, and inspection preparation, compare the article guidance with OSHA and DOT training support before changing internal workflows.

The same standard applies whether PHI sits on a server, a clipboard, a specimen label, or a prescription vial.

The Four HIPAA Rules Every Facility Must Know

HIPAA compliance is structured around four distinct rules administered by the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) through its Office for Civil Rights (OCR). Each rule answers a different question, and each carries its own penalties when ignored.

HIPAA Privacy Rule

The Privacy Rule, codified at 45 CFR Part 164 Subparts A and E, governs the use and disclosure of PHI in any form. It requires covered entities to apply administrative, technical, and physical safeguards to limit incidental and prohibited uses of PHI, and it explicitly extends those safeguards through final disposal.

For more detail on the same topic, review DOT Medical Waste Training: Requirements for Healthcare.

Workforce training, minimum necessary use, patient access rights, and Notices of Privacy Practices all live here.

The Privacy Rule is the rule most often invoked when patient records, prescription labels, or specimen containers end up in unsecured dumpsters.

HIPAA Security Rule

The Security Rule, at 45 CFR Part 164 Subpart C, applies only to electronic PHI (ePHI). It requires administrative safeguards (risk analysis, workforce training, sanction policies), physical safeguards (facility access controls, workstation security, media disposal), and technical safeguards (access controls, audit logs, transmission security).

On December 27, 2024, HHS issued a Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (NPRM) to overhaul the Security Rule for the first time since 2013, published in the Federal Register on January 6, 2025. The current Security Rule remains in effect while the proposed changes work through rulemaking.

HIPAA Breach Notification Rule

The Breach Notification Rule, at 45 CFR Part 164 Subpart D, sets timelines and content requirements for notifying individuals, HHS, and in some cases the media after a breach of unsecured PHI. Covered entities must notify affected individuals without unreasonable delay and no later than 60 calendar days after discovery of a breach.

Breaches affecting 500 or more residents of a state or jurisdiction also trigger notice to prominent media outlets within the same 60-day window. Smaller breaches can be logged and reported to OCR within 60 days after the end of the calendar year.

HIPAA Enforcement Rule

The Enforcement Rule, at 45 CFR Part 160 Subparts C, D, and E, gives OCR the authority to investigate complaints, conduct compliance reviews, and impose civil monetary penalties. Penalties are tiered by culpability and adjusted annually for inflation, with per-violation amounts ranging from $145 at the lowest tier to a per-violation cap above $2.1 million at the highest.

In 2025, the OCR resolved 21 HIPAA investigations with financial penalties, the second-highest annual total on record.

2025 to 2026 HIPAA Security Rule Update: What Is Actually Changing

The proposed Security Rule update is the most significant HIPAA cybersecurity development in more than a decade. According to HHS, the proposed rule would modify standards to better address increasing breaches and cyberattacks, remove deficiencies OCR has observed in compliance investigations, and align HIPAA with modern cybersecurity practices.

Key proposed changes include removing the distinction between “required” and “addressable” implementation specifications and making nearly all specifications required, mandating multi-factor authentication for access to ePHI, requiring encryption of ePHI at rest and in transit, requiring annually updated written asset inventories and network maps, and tightening incident response and contingency planning expectations.

The public comment period ran through March 7, 2025, drawing nearly 5,000 stakeholder comments, and the OCR has kept finalization of the rule on its official regulatory agenda for May 2026.

  • Important caveat: as of this writing, these are proposed changes, not law. The current Security Rule remains in effect. Facilities that wait until the final rule drops to begin asset inventory, MFA enforcement, and encryption work will face compressed implementation timelines. HHS has proposed a 180-day compliance period after any final rule’s effective date, with a longer transition window only for updating Business Associate Agreements.

HIPAA Compliance Checklist for Healthcare Facilities

A practical compliance program covers ten core obligations. Walk through these against your current operation and you will quickly see where the gaps are.

Conduct an Enterprise-Wide Risk Analysis

The Security Rule requires an accurate and thorough assessment of risks and vulnerabilities to the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of all ePHI. In 2025, 76% of OCR enforcement actions cited a risk analysis failure, making it by far the most common reason for a HIPAA fine. Risk analysis is not a one-time exercise. It needs to be repeated when systems change, when new technology is introduced, and at least annually.

Train the Workforce on HIPAA, Including Disposal

Every workforce member who handles or supervises the handling of PHI must be trained, including volunteers and temporary staff. Training must cover the entity’s specific policies and procedures, not just generic HIPAA awareness, and it must be documented.

Disposal procedures, breach reporting, and access controls deserve dedicated training time, because those are the areas OCR investigates most. Structured healthcare compliance training for employees closes the gap between knowing the rule and applying it at the bench, the front desk, and the loading dock.

Implement Administrative, Physical, and Technical Safeguards

Administrative safeguards include risk management, sanction policies, information access management, and contingency planning. Physical safeguards include facility access controls, workstation use policies, and device and media controls.

Technical safeguards include access controls, audit controls, integrity controls, and transmission security. The proposed 2025 Security Rule update would make nearly all of these mandatory, removing the previous flexibility some entities used to defer encryption and MFA.

Maintain Signed Business Associate Agreements

Establish Secure PHI Disposal Procedures

Document Chain of Custody and Destruction

Keep destruction logs that capture the date, method, description of the records destroyed, certification that destruction occurred, and signatures of personnel who supervised or witnessed it. Retain these for at least six years to align with HIPAA documentation requirements, or longer if state law or litigation holds require it. Certificates of destruction from vendors are part of the audit trail.

Run a Compliant Breach Response Program

Build written incident response procedures that cover detection, investigation, risk assessment, mitigation, notification, and post-incident review. Breach notification failures were the second most common reason for HIPAA financial penalties in 2025. Speed matters more than perfection: missing the 60-day notification deadline turns a security incident into a regulatory case.

Provide Patients with Access to Their Records

Patients have a federal right of access to their own PHI, and OCR has been enforcing this aggressively since 2019. Failures here are inexpensive to fix and disproportionately expensive to ignore.

Manage Vendors Continuously

A signed BAA is the start, not the finish. OCR’s 2025 enforcement signaled that covered entities are expected to vet vendors before signing, verify safeguards, monitor performance, and act on red flags. When a vendor breach happens, both the vendor and the covered entity are exposed.

Update Notices of Privacy Practices

Modifications to the Notice of Privacy Practices that were not vacated by the June 2025 Texas district court decision in Purl v. HHS remain in effect, with a compliance date of February 16, 2026. Practices need to confirm their NPPs reflect the surviving changes.

HIPAA Compliance Requirements for PHI Disposal and Medical Waste

This is where many healthcare facilities have their largest blind spot. The HIPAA Privacy and Security Rules do not prescribe a specific disposal method, but they do require that PHI be safeguarded all the way through final disposition. HHS has been explicit: covered entities are not permitted to abandon PHI or dispose of it in dumpsters or other containers accessible by the public or unauthorized persons.

What Counts as PHI in a Healthcare Waste Stream

PHI is any individually identifiable health information that links a person to their care, condition, or payment. In a waste stream, that means:

  • Printed medical records, lab reports, progress notes, and discharge summaries
  • Labeled prescription bottles and pharmacy vials
  • Hospital identification bracelets and patient wristbands
  • Specimen containers, lab vials, and pathology labels
  • IV bags, tubing, and packaging marked with patient identifiers
  • Appointment schedules, sign-in sheets, and billing documents
  • Imaging films and printed diagnostic results
  • Hard drives, USB drives, photocopier memory, fax machines, and other electronic media containing ePHI

If a patient's name, medical record number, date of birth, or other identifier is attached to a health-related fact on the item, it is PHI. A name alone on a calendar can be PHI when paired with the context of a clinical visit.

What Cannot Go in Regular Trash or Recycling

The 2022 OCR settlement with the New England Dermatology and Laser Center is the canonical example. The Massachusetts practice paid $300,640 and accepted a two-year corrective action plan after empty specimen containers labeled with patient names, dates of birth, and provider names were placed in an unsecured dumpster in the practice’s parking lot.

A passing security guard found one of the containers. CVS paid a $2.25 million settlement in 2009 for tossing prescription bottles and receipts in unsecured trash. Rite Aid paid $1 million for similar conduct in 2010. These are not theoretical risks.

PHI cannot be placed in public dumpsters, curbside recycling, open recycling bins, or any container accessible to unauthorized persons unless it has already been rendered unreadable. Mixing PHI with general trash does not solve the problem.

Approved Destruction Methods

For non-paper PHI like prescription bottles, wristbands, and specimen containers, equivalent destruction is required. The HHS-suggested approach is to maintain labeled bottles and similar items in opaque bags in a secure area and use a disposal vendor as a business associate to pick up and shred or otherwise destroy the items.

The BAA must address permitted uses, security obligations, breach notification timelines, subcontractor flow-down, audit rights, and return or destruction of PHI at the end of the relationship.

Facilities should also expect their disposal vendor to provide locked collection containers, documented chain of custody, GPS-tracked transport when off-site destruction is used, and certificates of destruction after each pickup.

HIPAA Vendor Checklist for Healthcare Facilities

A signed Business Associate Agreement that meets 45 CFR 164.504(e) content requirements

Written destruction procedures that meet the unreadable, indecipherable, irreconstructible standard

Locked, tamper-evident collection containers placed at point of generation

Chain-of-custody documentation from pickup to destruction

Certificates of destruction issued for each pickup, with date, method, and quantities

Documented employee background checks, training, and confidentiality agreements

Secure transport vehicles with route tracking

Verifiable references from other healthcare clients

Permits and licensure required by your state and the EPA

Adequate cyber and privacy liability insurance

Clear breach notification timelines that meet the 60-day HIPAA standard

A facility that hands a vendor a stack of records on a handshake is the facility that ends up writing a settlement check. A waste stream audit is the fastest way to map where PHI is generated, how it moves, and where the compliance gaps sit.

Common HIPAA Compliance Mistakes Facilities Make

Many violations are unforced errors. The same gaps appear over and over in OCR investigations, and almost all of them are inexpensive to fix:

  • Disposing of labeled prescription bottles, IV bags, and specimen containers in regular trash without removing or obscuring patient identifiers
  • Using an office shredder for high-volume PHI destruction without documenting the process
  • Leaving boxes of records in hallways, lobbies, or loading docks awaiting pickup
  • Failing to train workforce members involved in disposal, including overnight cleaning staff and temps
  • Skipping the annual risk analysis or treating it as a checklist exercise
  • Missing the 60-day breach notification window for affected individuals or HHS
  • Reusing or discarding old computers, copiers, USB drives, and mobile devices without certified data destruction
  • Allowing tracking pixels and third-party analytics on patient-facing pages that touch PHI paths
  • Forgetting to update Business Associate Agreements when scope, subcontractors, or services change
  • HIPAA Compliance and Workforce Training

HIPAA compliance lives or dies at the workforce level. Written policies do not protect a facility if the nurse, receptionist, lab tech, or pharmacy clerk handling PHI does not know what the policy says. The rules require training on the entity’s specific policies and procedures as necessary and appropriate for each workforce member, with refresher training when policies change.

Training should cover Privacy Rule basics, Security Rule safeguards, breach reporting, patient rights of access, disposal procedures, role-based access controls, and incident response. Documentation matters as much as content. In an OCR investigation, the facility is asked to produce training records, attendance, and assessment scores.

Investing in structured HIPAA compliance training gives healthcare facilities the audit trail they need, and tying it to related modules on bloodborne pathogens and OSHA training covers the full compliance picture for staff who handle regulated waste and patient information.

Building Sustainable HIPAA Compliance

HIPAA compliance is not a project with a finish line. It is an operating discipline that touches the front desk, the lab, the pharmacy, the IT stack, and the waste stream every single day. Facilities that treat compliance as a continuous program, with annual risk analyses, documented training, vetted vendors, and clear disposal procedures, are the ones that pass OCR investigations without writing settlement checks.

The proposed 2025 Security Rule changes signal where the regulatory bar is heading: more prescriptive cybersecurity controls, less flexibility, and more documentation. Healthcare organizations that build their compliance program around those expectations now will spend the next two years adjusting rather than scrambling.
