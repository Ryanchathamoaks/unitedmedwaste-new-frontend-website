How to Respond to Blood and Body Fluid Spills: A Step-by-Step Checklist

Blood and body fluid spills are an everyday reality in healthcare. What separates a safe, compliant response from a dangerous one is having a clear blood spill cleanup checklist in place before a spill ever happens.

This guide walks clinical and non-clinical staff through each step required under OSHA's Bloodborne Pathogens Standard, from area isolation and PPE selection through disinfection, disposal, and post-exposure reporting. Use it to train your team, build or update your written procedure, and stay ready for inspections.

What Counts as a Blood or Body Fluid Spill

OSHA's Bloodborne Pathogens Standard, codified at 29 CFR 1910.1030, operates under a principle called universal precautions: all human blood and certain other body fluids must be treated as if they are infectious for hepatitis B virus (HBV), hepatitis C virus (HCV), and HIV, regardless of the apparent health status of the source individual.

Beyond blood itself, the standard governs other potentially infectious materials (OPIM), which include semen, vaginal secretions, cerebrospinal fluid, synovial fluid, pleural fluid, amniotic fluid, saliva in dental procedures, and any fluid visibly contaminated with blood.

Sweat, tears, nasal secretions, urine, feces, and vomit are not covered by universal precautions unless they contain visible blood, though many facilities clean all body fluid spills with full precautions as a matter of infection control policy.

Any surface or item that has come into contact with blood or OPIM is considered contaminated under the standard and must be cleaned and decontaminated before reuse or removal. That includes floors, countertops, equipment surfaces, and any reusable containers.

What Your Blood Spill Kit Should Contain

A blood spill kit ensures staff can respond immediately without hunting for supplies. OSHA requires that spill cleanup materials be readily accessible wherever occupational exposure can reasonably be anticipated. The kit should include:

  • Spill boundary supplies: barrier tape, wet floor signs, or cones
  • Disposable nitrile or vinyl gloves (minimum two pairs per kit, since gloves are changed between the absorption and disinfection phases)
  • Disposable gown or apron that covers the torso and arms
  • Face shield or goggles for splash protection, plus a surgical mask
  • Shoe covers for large spills
  • Absorbent paper towels or absorbent granules to contain and collect bulk fluid
  • EPA-registered, tuberculocidal disinfectant or sodium hypochlorite solution (see dilution guidance in the procedure below)
  • Biohazard bags (red bags) with biohazard labeling, for all contaminated materials
  • Forceps or tongs for sharps removal, never bare hands
  • Incident report form or reference card with your facility's post-exposure reporting instructions

Kits should be inspected regularly and restocked immediately after use. A kit used and not replenished is a compliance gap waiting to be cited.

  • Blood and Body Fluid Spill Cleanup Procedure: Step by Step

This body fluid spill cleanup procedure aligns with OSHA 29 CFR 1910.1030 requirements and CDC environmental cleaning guidance. Only personnel who have received documented spill response training should perform cleanup. Do not assign untrained staff to handle blood or OPIM under any circumstances.

Step 1: Isolate and Restrict the Area

Act immediately. Prevent anyone from walking through the spill or approaching it before cleanup begins. For large spills, place barriers and wet floor signs around the perimeter. For small spills, direct bystanders away from the area and post a sign.

A second staff member standing watch while you gather supplies is a practical and effective option. Speed matters here because traffic through a contaminated area spreads the hazard and increases exposure risk.

Step 2: Don PPE Before Approaching the Spill

Put on all required PPE before making any contact with the spill or contaminated surfaces. This means:

  • Nitrile or vinyl gloves (wear a first pair now; you will change to a fresh pair before the disinfection phase)
  • Gown or disposable apron
  • Face shield or goggles if splashing is possible
  • Surgical or procedural mask
  • Shoe covers if the spill is large enough to risk contact with footwear

Gloves torn during cleanup must be changed immediately. Remove PPE in a way that does not contaminate your skin or clothing.

Step 3: Handle Any Sharps First

If the spill involves or is near contaminated sharps, broken glass, or needles, address those before absorbing any fluid. Use forceps or tongs. Never attempt to pick up a sharp with your hands, even while gloved. Place all sharps directly into a sharps disposal container. If a sharps container is not immediately at hand, use a rigid puncture-resistant interim container until proper disposal is possible.

Step 4: Absorb and Remove Bulk Fluid

Cover the spill with absorbent paper towels, blotting from the outer edges inward to avoid spreading the contamination. For larger spills, absorbent granules or powder can be applied directly to gel and solidify the fluid, making collection easier and reducing splash risk. Avoid wiping motions that drag fluid outward. Place all saturated absorbent material into a red biohazard bag. Do not seal the bag yet.

Change your gloves now. Remove the first pair carefully, rolling them inside out, and place them in the biohazard bag.

Step 5: Clean the Contaminated Surface

With fresh gloves on, wipe the area with clean absorbent material and neutral detergent and water to remove residual organic material. This cleaning step is not optional. Organic material (blood, tissue) can inactivate disinfectants if it is left on the surface before chemical application. The surface must be visibly clean before you apply any germicide.

Step 6: Disinfect With an Approved Germicide

Apply an EPA-registered, tuberculocidal disinfectant to the cleaned area. According to CDC guidelines, chlorine-based disinfectants at 500 to 5,000 ppm free chlorine are adequate for blood and body fluid spills on hard surfaces. This translates to a 1:100 dilution (small spills) or 1:10 dilution (large spills) of standard 5% household bleach.

Note: do not apply chlorine-based products to urine spills, and confirm your selected product is appropriate for the surface type being treated.

Allow the disinfectant to remain on the surface for the full contact time specified on the product label. Wipe away, then allow the surface to air dry. Skipping or shortening contact time is one of the most common cleanup errors, and it leaves pathogens intact despite the appearance of a clean surface.

Step 7: Dispose of All Contaminated Materials Correctly

All contaminated items used in the cleanup, paper towels, absorbent material, disposable gown, face shield if single-use, and both sets of gloves, must go into the biohazard bag. Once everything is collected, seal the bag securely. Double-bagging is appropriate if the outer surface of the first bag may be contaminated.

Biohazard bags must be closed prior to removal and must not be overfilled. Label or color-code regulated waste containers in accordance with 29 CFR 1910.1030(g)(1)(i). All regulated waste disposal must comply with applicable federal and state regulations. Step 8: Remove PPE and Wash Hands

Remove your gown first, rolling it inward to contain any contamination. Then remove your gloves. Wash your hands thoroughly with soap and water for a minimum of 20 seconds, even if you wore gloves throughout the entire procedure. Hand hygiene is mandatory after glove removal, not optional. Alcohol-based hand sanitizer is not a substitute when hands may have been exposed to blood or OPIM.

  • Post-Spill Actions: Reporting, Documentation, and Follow-Up

If any skin, eye, mouth, or mucous membrane contact with blood or OPIM occurred during the cleanup, that event is an exposure incident under 29 CFR 1910.1030. Exposure incidents require immediate reporting and documented follow-up.

Under OSHA's standard, employers must provide confidential medical evaluation and follow-up at no cost to the employee after any exposure incident. That evaluation includes documentation of the route of exposure, identification of the source individual where legally permissible, and serological testing for HIV and HBV.

Employers must also ensure post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) is offered when medically indicated, in line with current U.S. Public Health Service recommendations.

Unreported exposure incidents create legal and health risk for both the facility and the worker. NCBI research cited by 360training notes that a high percentage of healthcare workers fail to report exposure incidents, often because they lack clear reporting guidance or believe the exposure was low risk. A written procedure eliminates that ambiguity.

Medical records related to exposure incidents must be retained for the duration of employment plus 30 years. Training records must be retained for three years. Maintain these separately from personnel files and restrict access to protect confidentiality.

Common Spill Response Mistakes That Create Compliance Risk

Even well-run facilities fall into predictable errors during blood and body fluid spill response. The most common include:

Skipping PPE for small spills. No spill is too small to require gloves and eye protection. A few drops of blood on a countertop carry the same pathogen risk as a large pool. OSHA does not provide a volume threshold for precaution requirements.

Using the wrong disinfectant or skipping contact time. General-purpose cleaners are not tuberculocidal. Only EPA-registered products with documented efficacy against HBV and HIV qualify under the standard. Applying a disinfectant and immediately wiping it away does not count.

Disposing of contaminated materials in regular trash. Saturated absorbent materials, used gloves, and single-use PPE from a blood spill are regulated waste. They require biohazard containers, not standard waste bins. OSHA has issued numerous citations specifically for improper containerization of regulated waste.

Failing to report exposure incidents. Employees who experience contact with blood or OPIM during cleanup have a legal right to confidential post-exposure follow-up. Facilities must have a clear, documented reporting path so staff know exactly where to go and whom to notify immediately after an incident.

Assigning cleanup to untrained staff. Under 29 CFR 1910.1030, only trained and equipped personnel may handle potentially infectious materials. Assigning a new employee or volunteer to clean up a blood spill without documented training is a direct violation of the standard. This is why healthcare compliance training for employees needs to cover spill response explicitly, not as an afterthought.

Keeping Your Spill Response Procedure Audit-Ready

A blood spill cleanup procedure only protects your facility when it is documented, current, and tested. These four elements keep you inspection-ready:

Exposure Control Plan (ECP). Your written ECP must include spill response procedures, and OSHA requires it to be reviewed and updated at least annually. If your procedure still reflects outdated disinfectants or does not address OPIM beyond blood, it is overdue for revision.

Documented staff training. Every employee who may encounter a blood or body fluid spill must receive documented training before beginning spill-related duties and annually thereafter. Training must be interactive and content must reflect current procedures. A sign-in sheet alone does not satisfy the standard.

Spill kit inventory and restocking log. Maintain a schedule for inspecting kit contents and restocking used or expired supplies. An empty or incomplete spill kit is a compliance failure and a safety risk.

Incident and near-miss logging. Document every spill response, including those with no exposure. This creates a record that supports training updates, identifies recurring risk locations, and demonstrates due diligence if your facility is ever inspected.

Conclusion

A clear blood and body fluid spill cleanup procedure protects staff, patients, and your facility from both infection risk and regulatory consequences. The steps covered here, area isolation, PPE donning, sharps handling, absorption, cleaning, disinfection, proper disposal, hand hygiene, and incident reporting, form a complete blood spill response checklist that aligns directly with OSHA 29 CFR 1910.1030.

The procedure is only as strong as the training behind it. If your team has not completed structured bloodborne pathogens training recently, or if your Exposure Control Plan has not been reviewed and updated this year, those are the gaps most likely to surface during an inspection or after an incident.
