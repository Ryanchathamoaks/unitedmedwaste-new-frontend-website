Hazardous waste management for healthcare facilities in New England means following a layered set of federal and state rules that govern how waste is classified, stored, documented, and disposed of. The primary federal framework is the Resource Conservation and Recovery Act (RCRA), administered by the EPA.

Every New England state also operates its own EPA-authorized hazardous waste program, and those programs often go further than federal minimums.

United Medical Waste helps New England facilities connect regulatory requirements with practical service planning, including regulated medical waste pickup.

Most healthcare facilities generate at least some hazardous waste. Pharmaceutical waste, laboratory chemicals, disinfectants, and certain sharps-related waste streams all have specific compliance pathways. The rules differ based on how much waste a facility produces each month, what type of waste it is, and which state the facility is in.

Getting these details right protects staff, limits environmental liability, and keeps your facility out of trouble during audits or inspections.

This guide covers what counts as hazardous waste in a clinical setting, how the RCRA generator category system works, what New England states require beyond federal rules, and how to build a compliant hazardous waste program from the ground up.

What Qualifies as Hazardous Waste in a Healthcare Setting

Not all waste generated in a healthcare facility is hazardous waste. RCRA defines hazardous waste as solid waste that is either specifically listed by the EPA or exhibits one of four characteristics: ignitability, corrosivity, reactivity, or toxicity.

Healthcare facilities can generate all four types. The practical question is which waste streams at your specific facility fall under RCRA, versus which are regulated as infectious medical waste under state rules, versus which are ordinary solid waste.

A good starting point is reviewing the four categories of hazardous waste and then applying those definitions to the waste streams your facility actually produces.

For more detail on the same topic, review Biohazard Waste Disposal Boston: What Facilities Must Know.

Characteristic vs. Listed Wastes

Characteristic wastes are materials that fail one of the four hazard tests. Common healthcare examples include formaldehyde (toxic), acetone and methanol used in labs (ignitable), and certain cleaning solutions with extreme pH values (corrosive).

Listed wastes are materials the EPA has specifically named as hazardous, regardless of concentration. The lists most relevant to healthcare are:

  • F-list wastes: Spent solvents from non-specific sources, such as methylene chloride used in pathology labs
  • P-list and U-list wastes: Discarded commercial chemical products, which include many pharmaceutical compounds

P-list drugs are acutely hazardous. Even very small quantities trigger additional requirements. U-list drugs are generally hazardous, with less stringent thresholds. Examples of P-listed drugs include warfarin at certain concentrations, epinephrine, and several chemotherapy agents.

Common Hazardous Waste Streams in Clinical Environments

Facilities that do a thorough waste classification exercise often find more hazardous streams than expected. Common ones include:

  • Expired or unused P-listed and U-listed pharmaceuticals
  • Chemotherapy waste (both the drugs and contaminated materials)
  • Laboratory reagents and fixatives, including formaldehyde and xylene
  • Used developer and fixer solutions from imaging departments
  • Mercury-containing devices such as older thermometers and sphygmomanometers
  • Batteries, fluorescent lamps, and aerosol cans are managed under universal waste rules

Radioactive materials used in nuclear medicine are regulated separately under the Nuclear Regulatory Commission, not RCRA.

RCRA Generator Categories and What They Mean for Your Facility

Your RCRA generator category is determined each calendar month based on how much hazardous waste your facility produces. The category affects your storage time limits, inspection requirements, manifest obligations, and training requirements. It can change month to month if your waste generation fluctuates.

The three generator categories under RCRA are:

  • Very Small Quantity Generators (VSQG)

VSQGs generate 100 kg or less of non-acute hazardous waste per month and no more than 1 kg of acutely hazardous waste per month. Small outpatient clinics, dental offices, and single-provider practices often fall into this category.

Federal requirements for VSQGs are lighter than for other categories. There is no federal time limit on how long a VSQG can accumulate waste on-site, but total on-site accumulation must stay at or below 1,000 kg. Waste must be sent to a permitted treatment, storage, or disposal facility (TSDF), a licensed recycling facility, or consolidated at a large quantity generator under the same ownership.

Note that VSQGs may still need an EPA Identification Number in some states. Massachusetts requires all generators to obtain an EPA ID through the RCRAinfo system; the old state-only generator ID option was eliminated in the state's May 2024 regulation update.

Small Quantity Generators (SQG)

SQGs generate more than 100 kg but less than 1,000 kg of non-acute hazardous waste per month. Many mid-sized practices, surgery centers, and specialty clinics fall here.

SQGs must obtain an EPA ID, use manifests for all hazardous waste shipments, store waste in properly labeled and inspected containers, and limit on-site accumulation to 6,000 kg. Storage time is capped at 180 days (or 270 days if the waste is transported more than 200 miles for disposal).

SQGs must notify the EPA of their hazardous waste activities every four years through the RCRAinfo system. They also need a designated emergency coordinator and must post emergency information near accumulation areas.

Large Quantity Generators (LQG)

LQGs generate 1,000 kg or more of non-acute hazardous waste per month, or more than 1 kg per month of acutely hazardous waste. Hospitals, multi-specialty group practices, large reference labs, and biotech facilities frequently qualify as LQGs.

LQGs face the most stringent requirements. Waste must leave the facility within 90 days of first entering the main hazardous waste storage area. LQGs must inspect accumulation areas weekly, maintain a written contingency plan, conduct formal employee training on hazardous waste handling, and submit a Biennial Report to the EPA for all waste generated during any year they hold LQG status.

LQGs must also register in the EPA's e-Manifest system and designate a Certifier or Site Manager. Since January 22, 2025, this registration requirement has been in active enforcement.

  • Satellite Accumulation Areas: Rules and Best Practices

A satellite accumulation area (SAA) is a space at or near the point of waste generation where staff can temporarily collect hazardous waste before transferring it to the main storage area. A hospital pharmacy, an oncology suite, or a clinical lab bench may all qualify as SAAs.

SAAs are useful because waste accumulated there does not count toward your monthly generator total until it leaves the SAA. This can help facilities manage their generator status and avoid stepping into a higher compliance tier unnecessarily.

The rules for SAAs are specific:

  • One practical point: the SAA limit is per satellite location, not per waste type. If you have a lab bench with two separate waste containers, one for solvents and one for a different chemical stream, you still cannot exceed 55 gallons in total at that location.
  • No more than 55 gallons of hazardous waste (or 1 quart of acutely hazardous waste) may be accumulated at a single SAA at any time
  • Containers must be in good condition, compatible with the waste, and kept closed except when adding waste
  • Each container must be labeled with the words "Hazardous Waste" and include a description of the contents
  • Once the 55-gallon limit is reached, the waste must be moved to the main storage area within three days

Staff who manage SAAs need to understand what goes where, how to label containers correctly, and when to transfer waste. These are trainable tasks, but they require a written procedure and consistent reinforcement.

  • Hazardous Pharmaceutical Waste: RCRA Subpart P Requirements

The EPA's 40 CFR Part 266 Subpart P introduced specific standards for hazardous waste pharmaceuticals at healthcare facilities. The rule applies to facilities generating hazardous pharmaceutical waste above the VSQG threshold, as well as reverse distributors handling potentially creditable pharmaceuticals.

The most important provision in Subpart P is the nationwide ban on sewering. No hazardous waste pharmaceuticals may be poured down a drain, toilet, or sink. This applies to all listed hazardous pharmaceuticals regardless of concentration or remaining volume.

Other key Subpart P requirements for healthcare generators include:

  • Classifying all pharmaceutical waste to distinguish between creditable and non-creditable hazardous waste
  • Using containers specifically designated for hazardous pharmaceutical waste, with proper labeling
  • Tracking accumulation dates and ensuring disposal within the 365-day storage limit
  • Registering with the EPA and filing notification of hazardous waste activities

For facilities with significant pharmaceutical waste streams, it is worth reviewing pharmaceutical waste solutions that address both the disposal and the documentation side of Subpart P compliance.

The distinction between hazardous pharmaceutical waste and non-hazardous pharmaceutical waste matters practically because they follow different disposal paths. Hazardous pharmaceuticals require a RCRA manifest and disposal at a licensed TSDF.

Non-hazardous medications may be eligible for reverse distribution or non-incineration disposal methods, depending on the state.

New England State-Specific Hazardous Waste Requirements

All six New England states operate EPA-authorized hazardous waste programs under RCRA. That means each state has its own regulations that are at least as stringent as federal requirements, and often more so. Facilities operating across multiple states need to track each state's rules independently.

For a broader overview of regional disposal rules, the New England waste disposal regulations guide covers state-by-state differences across the region.

Massachusetts updated its hazardous waste regulations (310 CMR 30.000) on May 24, 2024. Changes included adopting the federal e-Manifest system, adding aerosol cans to the universal waste rule, and eliminating the state-only generator ID option. All Massachusetts generators must now obtain an EPA ID number through RCRAinfo.

Massachusetts also requires that generators retain a copy of all test results, waste analyses, and relevant findings for at least ten years after the waste was last sent for disposal. The Massachusetts Department of Environmental Protection (MassDEP) received final federal authorization for these program revisions, which became effective November 4, 2025.

Connecticut requires a Uniform Hazardous Waste Manifest for certain medical waste categories. Generators must send a copy of the signed manifest to the state commissioner within seven days of a transporter accepting the waste.

Containers with ignitable or reactive waste must be kept at least 50 feet from treatment facilities, and storage areas must be inspected weekly with documented results.

Rhode Island, Vermont, Maine, and New Hampshire each have state-specific rules on waste classification, storage, reporting, and transporter licensing. New Hampshire, for example, requires that regulated medical waste be autoclaved or incinerated before landfill disposal.

The practical implication for multi-site healthcare organizations: a compliance protocol that works in one New England state may not fully satisfy requirements in another. A qualified compliance partner with knowledge of all six state programs can help prevent gaps.

Hazardous Waste Manifests and Recordkeeping

A hazardous waste manifest is the paper trail that follows your waste from the point of generation to its final disposal. It identifies the generator, the transporter, the waste type and quantity, and the receiving facility. Every SQG and LQG must use a manifest for all regulated hazardous waste shipments.

The EPA's e-Manifest system digitizes this process. As of January 22, 2025, LQGs and SQGs are required to register in e-Manifest and designate a Certifier or Site Manager.

The system tracks waste shipments electronically and reduces paperwork burden, but facilities that have not yet registered are out of compliance. The RCRA hazardous waste regulations at 40 CFR Parts 260 through 273 detail the full manifest and recordkeeping requirements.

Key recordkeeping rules for healthcare generators:

  • Documentation protects your facility in three scenarios: regulatory inspection, billing disputes with your waste vendor, and civil liability if a release occurs during transport. If you cannot produce a manifest chain from generation to disposal, you cannot demonstrate cradle-to-grave compliance.
  • LQGs and SQGs must keep signed copies of all manifests for at least three years from the date the waste was accepted by the initial transporter
  • If a signed copy of the manifest is not returned within 35 days of shipment, the generator must contact the transporter and the disposal facility to determine the status of the shipment
  • LQGs must submit a Biennial Report to the EPA by March 1 of every even-numbered year, covering all hazardous waste generated during the previous calendar year
  • Massachusetts requires ten-year retention of test results and waste analyses, which is more stringent than the federal three-year minimum
  • Staff Training for Hazardous Waste Handling

Training is one of the areas where healthcare facilities most frequently fall short. LQGs must conduct personnel training to ensure all employees who manage hazardous waste can perform their duties without threatening human health or the environment. Training must be completed within six months of hire and repeated annually. Training records must be kept on-site for the current staff member plus three years after departure.

SQGs have a less formal training requirement but must still ensure employees are familiar with emergency procedures and proper waste handling. VSQGs have no explicit federal training mandate, but state rules and OSHA requirements still apply.

RCRA compliance training covers the generator-specific requirements your staff need to understand, including waste identification, container management, labeling standards, and emergency response procedures.

It is worth distinguishing this from OSHA training on bloodborne pathogens, which covers a different set of risks. Both are needed. Neither substitutes for the other.

The most common training failure is treating hazardous waste compliance as a one-time orientation topic rather than an ongoing operational practice. When staff rotate or procedures change, training gaps appear quickly.

How to Choose a Licensed Hazardous Waste Hauler in New England

The generator is legally responsible for their waste from cradle to grave. That responsibility does not transfer to the hauler when the container leaves your facility. If your transporter or disposal facility is not properly licensed, the liability can flow back to you.

Before contracting with any hazardous waste vendor, verify:

  • The transporter holds a current hazardous waste transporter license in your state (or each state they operate in)
  • The receiving treatment, storage, or disposal facility has a valid EPA permit and state authorization
  • The vendor uses the e-Manifest system and can provide compliant manifests for every pickup
  • The hauler carries adequate liability insurance
  • The vendor can provide documentation of final disposal, not just transport

If you want to understand your waste streams before selecting a vendor, you can request a free waste stream audit to identify exactly what you generate, which categories it falls into, and what the correct disposal pathway is for each stream.

Smaller healthcare facilities sometimes discover through an audit that they have been over-classifying waste or routing it through the wrong channel, both of which add cost and compliance exposure unnecessarily. An audit also establishes a documented baseline that helps if you are ever subject to an inspection.

Understanding and managing hazardous RCRA wastes in healthcare settings goes a long way in helping facilities ask the right questions of any vendor they are considering.

Conclusion

Hazardous waste management for healthcare facilities in New England requires more than just arranging pickup. It requires understanding what you generate, which RCRA category you fall into, how to store and document waste correctly, and how each of the six New England states layers requirements on top of federal minimums.

Misclassified waste, missed manifests, and undertrained staff are the most common sources of compliance exposure, and they are all preventable with the right program in place.
