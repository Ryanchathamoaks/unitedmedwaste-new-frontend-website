What Is OPIM? Meaning, Examples & Waste Disposal

OPIM stands for Other Potentially Infectious Materials. See the OSHA definition, examples, healthcare risks, handling rules, and safe disposal steps.

OPIM stands for Other Potentially Infectious Materials. Under OSHA's bloodborne pathogens standard, OPIM includes certain human body fluids, unfixed human tissue or organs, and specific HIV- or HBV-containing cultures, solutions, blood, organs, or tissues that may create occupational exposure to bloodborne pathogens.

In healthcare settings, OPIM matters because it affects exposure control plans, employee training, PPE, labeling, sharps handling, regulated waste containment, and disposal procedures.

  • OPIM Meaning: What Does OPIM Stand For?

OPIM stands for Other Potentially Infectious Materials. The term comes from OSHA's bloodborne pathogens standard, where it names every infectious material beyond human blood that can expose a worker to bloodborne pathogens on the job.

When reviewing red bag waste, containers, pickup records, and service scheduling, compare the article guidance with medical waste disposal services before changing internal workflows.

The phrase does real work in a clinical setting. "Other" signals that blood already has its own category. "Potentially infectious" means a material does not need a confirmed pathogen to count. If it could carry a bloodborne pathogen and a worker could be exposed, it falls under OPIM rules.

Staff see the acronym most often in three places: the facility's exposure control plan, bloodborne pathogens training, and the labels on regulated waste containers. Each of those uses the same OSHA definition, so getting the meaning right keeps a clinic consistent across training, PPE, and proper medical waste management.

OSHA Definition of OPIM

OSHA defines OPIM in the bloodborne pathogens standard at 29 CFR 1910.1030. The definition has three parts, and a material qualifies if it fits any one of them.

For more detail on the same topic, review Biohazard Waste Disposal Boston: What Facilities Must Know.

First, a fixed list of human body fluids: semen, vaginal secretions, cerebrospinal fluid, synovial fluid, pleural fluid, pericardial fluid, peritoneal fluid, amniotic fluid, and saliva in dental procedures. The list also covers any body fluid visibly contaminated with blood and any situation where it is difficult or impossible to tell one body fluid from another.

Second, any unfixed tissue or organ, other than intact skin, taken from a human, living or dead.

Third, laboratory and research materials: HIV-containing cell or tissue cultures, organ cultures, HIV- or HBV-containing culture medium or other solutions, and blood, organs, or other tissues from experimental animals infected with HIV or HBV.

In plain English, OPIM is the catchall OSHA built so that infectious materials beyond blood do not slip through the cracks. A worker who handles any of these in the course of their job is covered by the same protections that apply to blood: training, PPE, labeling, and compliant disposal.

OPIM Examples in Healthcare

OPIM shows up across almost every clinical department, not just the lab or the OR. The examples below come straight from the OSHA list and the daily realities of patient care.

  • Body fluids: cerebrospinal fluid from a spinal tap, synovial fluid from a joint aspiration, pleural and pericardial fluid from chest procedures, peritoneal fluid from abdominal taps, and amniotic fluid in labor and delivery.
  • Reproductive fluids: semen and vaginal secretions handled in fertility, obstetric, and gynecologic care.
  • Dental saliva: saliva is OPIM specifically during dental procedures, where blood mixing is likely.
  • Blood-contaminated fluids: any fluid you can see is mixed with blood, such as wound drainage or fluid in a suction canister.
  • Indistinguishable fluids: spills or specimens where you cannot reliably separate one body fluid from another.
  • Tissue and organs: unfixed surgical specimens, biopsy tissue, and pathology samples, but not intact skin.
  • Research materials: HIV cultures, HBV culture media, and infected animal blood or tissue in clinical and biotech labs.

Treating each of these as OPIM drives the right downstream choices on gloves, gowns, containers, and waste segregation.

The distinction matters for clarity, not for handling. Blood, blood components, and products made from human blood get the full set of bloodborne pathogens controls on their own. OPIM exists so that materials beyond blood receive identical treatment. When you read OSHA documents, the phrase "blood or OPIM" appears constantly because the two categories almost always carry the same requirements for PPE, labeling, and disposal.

Saliva is OPIM only in dental procedures. OSHA singled out dental saliva because dental work routinely draws blood, so saliva in that setting is treated as potentially infectious.

Outside of dental procedures, plain saliva is not automatically OPIM. The exception is saliva that is visibly contaminated with blood, which qualifies under the broader rule for blood-contaminated fluids. For dental practices, the practical effect is simple: treat suctioned fluids, contaminated instruments, and saliva-soaked materials as regulated material and route them accordingly.

Vomit, urine, feces, sweat, tears, and nasal secretions are not OPIM on their own. OSHA deliberately left them off the body fluid list because they do not normally transmit bloodborne pathogens.

Two conditions change that. If any of these fluids is visibly contaminated with blood, it becomes OPIM. And if a spill mixes fluids so you cannot tell one from another, the whole mixture is treated as OPIM. The rule rewards a cautious read: when in doubt about blood content, handle the material as OPIM and use regulated waste procedures.

How OPIM Relates to Bloodborne Pathogens

OPIM is the reason the bloodborne pathogens standard reaches beyond blood. Pathogens such as HIV, hepatitis B, and hepatitis C can live in the body fluids, tissue, and cultures the OPIM definition covers, so OSHA extends the same occupational protections to all of them.

That connection drives a facility's entire safety program. The exposure control plan must identify which jobs involve contact with blood or OPIM. Those workers need bloodborne pathogens training, the right PPE, and access to hepatitis B vaccination. Sharps that have touched OPIM go into puncture-resistant containers, and any post-exposure incident triggers the standard's evaluation and follow-up steps.

Get the OPIM scope right and every other control falls into place.

How OPIM Should Be Handled and Disposed Of

OPIM waste must be contained, labeled, and disposed of under OSHA's regulated waste rules plus applicable state and local requirements. Not every item that touches OPIM becomes regulated medical waste, so classification depends on the waste type, its condition, and your state's rules.

OSHA's regulated waste provisions set clear container standards. Containers for regulated waste must be closable, must be constructed to contain contents and prevent leakage during handling and transport, must be labeled or color-coded, and must be closed before removal to prevent spillage.

Contaminated sharps go into containers that are closable, puncture-resistant, leak-proof on the sides and bottom, and labeled or color-coded.

A practical handling sequence at the point of care looks like this:

Segregate at the source. Separate regulated waste from general trash the moment it is generated.

Use the right container. Sharps into sharps containers, soft regulated waste into labeled or red bags, fluids into approved leak-proof containers.

Label or color-code. Apply the biohazard label or red color-coding the standard requires.

Close before transport. Seal containers so nothing spills during movement or hauling.

Dispose through a compliant route. Use a licensed medical waste hauler and treatment process that meets federal, state, and local rules.

Because state medical waste rules vary across New England, the exact bag, container, and tracking requirements differ between Massachusetts, Rhode Island, and Connecticut. A compliant program follows the strictest applicable standard for each site. Facilities that need help can arrange medical waste disposal built around their specific waste streams and state requirements.

OPIM becomes regulated medical waste when it meets OSHA's definition of regulated waste, but not before. OSHA defines regulated waste as liquid or semi-liquid blood or OPIM, items contaminated with blood or OPIM that would release these substances if compressed, items caked with dried blood or OPIM that could release them during handling, contaminated sharps, and pathological or microbiological wastes containing blood or OPIM.

The takeaway for facilities is that condition matters. A lightly soiled item that would not release fluid may not meet the regulated waste threshold, while a saturated dressing or a sharp one clearly does. This is why containerization and classification depend on the waste's state, the waste type, and your state's medical waste regulations rather than the mere presence of the OPIM label.

Matching each item to the correct route avoids both undertreatment, which is a compliance risk, and overtreatment, which wastes money.

OPIM Training Requirements for Healthcare Staff

Any employee with reasonably anticipated contact with blood or OPIM must receive bloodborne pathogens training before starting those tasks and at least annually after that. OSHA requires the training under the same standard that defines OPIM.

The training has to cover the standard itself, how bloodborne pathogens spread, the facility's exposure control plan, recognition of tasks that involve OPIM, proper use of PPE, the hepatitis B vaccine, and the steps to follow after an exposure incident. New procedures or new container systems can trigger additional training.

Documented, current training is one of the first things an inspector checks, and it is the most common gap in small practices. Facilities can close that gap with structured compliance training aligned to the bloodborne pathogens standard.
