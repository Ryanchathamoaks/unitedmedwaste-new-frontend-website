Exposure Control Plan Review: OSHA Annual Update Guide

OSHA exposure control plans must be reviewed & updated at least annually. Learn what the annual review must document & which changes force an update.

Back to Insights Call 508-234-2400

How Often Should Exposure Control Plans Be Reviewed and Updated?

An exposure control plan must be reviewed and updated at least once a year. It also has to be updated any time tasks, procedures, or job positions change in a way that affects workplace exposure to blood or other infectious materials. The annual review is the floor, not the ceiling. If something changes mid-year, the plan cannot wait until its next scheduled check.

That single rule answers most of what people search for. The plan is a living document, reviewed on a fixed annual schedule and revised on demand whenever the work itself shifts. A plan written once and left in a binder is out of compliance the moment conditions change, even if a year has not passed.

This guidance comes straight from the federal Bloodborne Pathogens Standard, 29 CFR 1910.1030, enforced by the Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA). The standard has applied to covered employers since 1992, with sharps-safety provisions added under the Needlestick Safety and Prevention Act that took effect in 2001.

The core review requirement has not changed since then, but how strictly it gets enforced has stayed front and center.

When reviewing staff training, recordkeeping, and inspection preparation, compare the article guidance with OSHA and DOT training support before changing internal workflows.

OSHA enforcement records from October 2013 through August 2025 show that failing to establish a written Exposure Control Plan is the single most frequently cited bloodborne pathogens violation, and failing to review and update that plan at least annually ranks fourth.

  • OSHA Exposure Control Plan Review: The Short Answer

Under 29 CFR 1910.1030, every employer whose workers face occupational exposure to blood or other potentially infectious materials must keep a written Exposure Control Plan and review and update it at least annually.

The plan must also change whenever new or modified tasks, procedures, or employee positions affect exposure, and it must reflect new technology that reduces bloodborne pathogen risk. That covers hospitals, clinics, dental offices, labs, tattoo studios, and any other workplace where staff can reasonably expect contact with blood.

The phrase "at least annually" is doing a lot of work. It sets a minimum. Many facilities review more often because their procedures, staffing, or devices change throughout the year. The review is also more than a calendar formality. OSHA expects each annual review to document specific decisions, which the next sections break down.

What OSHA Requires in a Written Exposure Control Plan

A written Exposure Control Plan is the document that spells out how an employer will eliminate or minimize worker exposure to bloodborne pathogens such as HIV, hepatitis B, and hepatitis C. It is not optional paperwork. It is the foundation OSHA inspects first, because the written plan controls everything downstream: training, vaccination, controls, and incident response.

The standard requires the plan to contain a defined set of elements. Each one is reviewed during the annual check, so knowing the parts tells you what the review actually covers. At minimum, the plan must include an exposure determination, a schedule and method for implementing the standard's compliance sections, and a procedure for evaluating the circumstances around any exposure incident.

The employer must also keep a copy accessible to employees and make it available to OSHA on request.

Exposure determination

The exposure determination is the part of the plan that names who is at risk. It is built without regard to whether workers wear personal protective equipment, so it measures real exposure, not exposure after gloves and gowns. The standard requires the determination to list three things:

All job classifications in which every employee has occupational exposure, such as phlebotomists or surgical nurses.

Job classifications in which only some employees have occupational exposure, such as housekeeping staff who occasionally handle contaminated waste.

All tasks and procedures, or closely related groups of them, where occupational exposure occurs and is performed by the partly exposed classifications.

If a facility adds a new role or shifts duties between roles, the exposure determination is one of the first sections that goes stale. That is a common trigger for a mid-year update.

Methods of compliance

This section ties the plan to the actual safeguards on the floor. OSHA organizes protection into a hierarchy: universal precautions treat all blood as infectious, engineering controls remove or isolate the hazard, work practice controls change how tasks are performed, and personal protective equipment forms the last layer.

Engineering controls include sharps disposal containers, self-sheathing needles, needleless systems, and other devices with engineered sharps injury protections. The plan documents which controls the facility uses and the schedule for putting them in place.

How Often Must an Exposure Control Plan Be Updated?

An Exposure Control Plan must be updated on two separate clocks. The first is fixed: at least once every 12 months. The second is event-driven: any time a change in the workplace affects exposure risk. Both clocks run at the same time, and the event-driven one can force an update long before the annual date arrives.

This dual requirement is where many facilities slip. They treat the annual review as the only obligation and skip the in-between updates that the standard plainly requires.

Annual review requirement

The plan must be reviewed and updated at least annually. That review is mandatory even in a quiet year. A facility that changed nothing, hired no one, and bought no new devices still has to conduct and document the review. The point of the annual cycle is to force a deliberate look at the plan rather than letting it drift.

When updates are required before the annual review

The standard requires an update whenever necessary to reflect new or modified tasks and procedures that affect occupational exposure, and to reflect new or revised employee positions with occupational exposure. In practice, that means an update is due when any of the following happen:

The facility adds a procedure that creates exposure, such as starting in-house lab draws.

An existing procedure changes in a way that alters exposure, such as switching to a new instrument.

A new job classification with exposure is created, or duties move between roles.

New technology becomes available that reduces bloodborne pathogen exposure.

An exposure incident reveals a gap the current plan does not address.

If any of these occur, waiting for the annual review is not compliant. The update happens when the change happens.

What the Annual Review Must Document

The annual review is not satisfied by re-signing last year's plan. OSHA requires the review to document specific decisions about technology and devices. This is the part competitors' answer pages often skip, and it is exactly where inspectors look.

The standard requires the review and update to reflect changes in technology that eliminate or reduce exposure, and to document, every year, the employer's consideration and implementation of appropriate commercially available and effective safer medical devices. In short, the facility must show its work: which safer devices it looked at, and what it decided to adopt or reject and why.

Safer medical device review

Each year the employer has to evaluate safer medical devices on the market, not just confirm that the ones already in use still exist. Safer devices include sharps with engineered injury protections and needleless systems. The review documents this evaluation in writing.

A plan that has not mentioned a device decision in years is a red flag, and OSHA enforcement data confirms it: failing to document annual consideration of safer medical devices is among the most frequently cited provisions of the standard.

Input from non-managerial employees

The standard requires employers to solicit input from non-managerial employees who provide direct patient care and who face exposure to contaminated sharps. These are the people who use the devices, so their feedback shapes which engineering and work practice controls actually work. The employer must document that this input was requested.

A signed acknowledgment, a meeting record, or a survey of frontline staff all serve as evidence. Skipping this step is its own citable violation.

Exposure Control Plan Review Checklist

A focused review keeps the annual check from becoming a rubber stamp. Use the following checklist to confirm the plan still matches the workplace it governs:

Confirm the written plan is current, dated, and signed for this review cycle.

Verify the exposure determination lists every current job classification and task with exposure.

Document the evaluation of commercially available safer medical devices for this year.

Record solicited input from non-managerial direct-care staff on sharps controls.

Check that the implementation schedule for engineering and work practice controls reflects current practice.

Confirm the exposure incident evaluation procedure is accurate and accessible.

Verify the plan remains accessible to employees during all working hours.

Records to Check During the Review

The plan review is also a records review. Several documents tie back to the plan, and gaps in them signal that the plan is not being followed even if the plan itself reads well. Pull these records during the annual check:

Training records. Workers must be trained at initial assignment and at least annually after that, with each annual session falling within one year of the previous one. Missing or late training is a frequent citation.

Hepatitis B vaccination documentation. Confirm vaccinations were offered, and that any declinations are signed.

Exposure incident documentation. Verify that each incident was evaluated under the plan's procedure.

Medical records. Confirm they are maintained and kept confidential.

The sharps injury log. For employers required to keep an injury and illness log, the sharps injury log records the type and brand of device involved, the department or work area where the incident occurred, and an explanation of how it happened.

The sharps injury log is more than a recordkeeping chore. Patterns in it, such as repeated injuries from one device, are exactly the evidence the annual safer-device review is supposed to act on.

How External Guidance Can Force a Mid-Year Update

The plan does not exist in a vacuum. When national clinical guidance changes, the exposure incident procedure inside the plan can fall out of date before the annual review comes around. A 2025 update to the federal guidelines for managing occupational HIV exposures revised post-exposure prophylaxis regimens, shortened the follow-up testing window, and removed some routine lab tests.

A facility whose plan still points staff to the older follow-up protocol now has a procedure that no longer matches current practice.

This is the "whenever necessary" clause in action. A change in technology or clinical standard is grounds for an immediate update, not a note to revisit next year. Treating outside guidance shifts as update triggers keeps the plan accurate between annual reviews.

Common Mistakes When Reviewing Exposure Control Plans

The most common failures are not exotic. They are predictable shortcuts that inspectors see repeatedly. Avoid these:

Reusing an unchanged template. A generic plan that never names the facility's own job classifications, tasks, or devices is treated as no real plan at all.

Skipping the safer-device evaluation. Confirming current devices still work is not the same as evaluating what is newly available.

Forgetting employee input. Failing to solicit and document frontline staff feedback is a standalone violation.

Missing the annual cycle in a quiet year. No changes does not mean no review. The annual review is still required.

Letting the exposure determination lag. New roles and shifted duties make last year's determination inaccurate fast.

The financial stakes are real. For 2026, OSHA can assess up to $16,550 per serious violation and up to $165,514 for willful or repeat violations, and a single plan can generate several separate citations across its required elements.

How Exposure Control Plan Reviews Connect to Training

A plan review and a training cycle reinforce each other. The standard requires bloodborne pathogens training at initial assignment and at least annually, and that annual training has to happen within a year of the prior session. When the plan changes, training content should change with it, because employees are trained on the plan as written.

Workers also have the right to access the written plan, so an updated plan needs to reach the people it governs, not just the compliance file. Pairing the annual plan review with the annual training refresh keeps both on the same schedule and closes the gap between what the plan says and what staff actually know.
