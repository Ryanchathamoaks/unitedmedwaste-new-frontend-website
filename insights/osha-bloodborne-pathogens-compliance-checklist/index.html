OSHA Bloodborne Pathogens Compliance Checklist

Use this OSHA bloodborne pathogens compliance checklist to meet 29 CFR 1910.1030. Covers ECPs, training, PPE, HBV vaccination, and recordkeeping.

Back to Insights
  • OSHA Bloodborne Pathogens Compliance Checklist: 10 Steps to Meet 29 CFR 1910.1030

The OSHA bloodborne pathogens compliance checklist covers the ten required employer actions under 29 CFR 1910.1030, the federal standard that protects workers from exposure to hepatitis B (HBV), hepatitis C (HCV), human immunodeficiency virus (HIV), and other pathogens present in blood and body fluids.

Every employer whose workers have any reasonably anticipated contact with blood or other potentially infectious materials (OPIM) must meet this standard, regardless of facility size or specialty.

United Medical Waste helps New England facilities connect regulatory requirements with practical service planning, including healthcare compliance training.

This guide walks through each requirement in plain language, with the exact regulatory citation for each step. Use it to audit your current program, prepare for an OSHA inspection, or build your compliance documentation from scratch.

Why OSHA BBP Compliance Matters for Healthcare Employers

Approximately 385,000 needlestick injuries occur in U.S. hospitals each year, and the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) estimates that 62 to 88 percent of those injuries are preventable with proper engineering controls and trained staff.

OSHA estimates that 5.6 million healthcare and related workers face occupational exposure to bloodborne pathogens through sharps injuries and contact with contaminated materials.

When reviewing staff training, recordkeeping, and inspection preparation, compare the article guidance with OSHA and DOT training support before changing internal workflows.

The financial stakes are just as significant as the health risks. Serious violations of the Bloodborne Pathogens Standard carry fines starting at $16,550 per instance. Willful or repeat violations can reach $165,514 per citation, and failure-to-abate penalties accrue at up to $14,502 per day until the violation is corrected.

OSHA's enforcement data from October 2013 through August 2025 shows a consistent pattern: the violations cited most often are administrative, not accidental. Missing written plans, outdated records, and untrained staff are responsible for the majority of citations. The checklist below addresses each of those gaps directly.

The OSHA Bloodborne Pathogens Compliance Checklist

Step 1: Write and Maintain an Exposure Control Plan

  • Regulatory citation: 29 CFR 1910.1030(c)

The Exposure Control Plan (ECP) is the single most-cited element in OSHA's Bloodborne Pathogens Standard (29 CFR 1910.1030) enforcement history. Every employer covered by the standard must have a written ECP that:

For more detail on the same topic, review DOT Medical Waste Training: Requirements for Healthcare.

  • Identifies every job classification and specific task with occupational exposure
  • Describes the engineering controls, work practice controls, and PPE the employer will use
  • Sets out the schedule for implementing each element of the standard
  • Documents that the employer considered and implemented appropriate safer medical devices

The ECP must be accessible to all employees during their shifts. Storing it only in a locked administrator's office does not satisfy OSHA's accessibility requirement.

  • A critical requirement that many facilities miss: the ECP must be reviewed and updated at least annually and whenever new tasks, procedures, or job positions with exposure are added. OSHA also requires employers to document that they solicited input from non-managerial employees who perform direct patient care when selecting engineering controls and safer devices. That solicitation must appear in the written plan.
  • Checklist item: Your ECP exists in writing, identifies all exposed roles and tasks, is accessible to employees, was reviewed within the last 12 months, and contains documentation of safer-device consideration and non-managerial input.

Step 2: Determine Which Employees Have Occupational Exposure

  • Regulatory citation: 29 CFR 1910.1030(c)(2)

Exposure determination identifies who in your facility faces reasonably anticipated contact with blood or OPIM as part of their normal duties. This is not limited to clinical staff. The exposure determination must include:

  • Category I employees: Workers who perform tasks with certain or predictable exposure (nurses, phlebotomists, surgical technicians, clinical lab workers, housekeeping staff who handle regulated waste)
  • Category II employees: Workers whose duties do not routinely involve exposure but may involve unplanned contact (administrative staff who may assist in emergencies)

The exposure determination must be made without regard to the use of PPE. If a task involves blood contact without gloves, that task must be listed even if the worker normally wears gloves. The determination is about the task, not the protection.

  • Checklist item: Your ECP lists all job titles and specific tasks with occupational exposure and classifies each correctly under Category I or Category II.

Step 3: Apply Universal Precautions

  • Regulatory citation: 29 CFR 1910.1030(d)(1)

Universal precautions require employers and employees to treat all human blood and OPIM as if they are known to be infectious for HIV, HBV, HCV, and other bloodborne pathogens. There is no exception for patients with known negative status. This approach applies to the following body fluids as defined by the standard:

  • Blood and blood-contaminated body fluids
  • Semen and vaginal secretions
  • Cerebrospinal, synovial, pleural, pericardial, peritoneal, and amniotic fluids
  • Saliva during dental procedures
  • Any body fluid where differentiation is difficult or impossible

Universal precautions do not apply to urine, feces, nasal secretions, or sweat unless visibly contaminated with blood. OSHA's BBP standard requires employers to train staff on this distinction and enforce it through written work practice controls.

  • Checklist item: Your written policies and healthcare compliance training program explicitly require universal precautions for all blood and OPIM.

Step 4: Implement Engineering Controls

  • Regulatory citation: 29 CFR 1910.1030(d)(2)

Engineering controls are the primary line of defense under 29 CFR 1910.1030. They physically eliminate or minimize the exposure hazard without relying on employee behavior. Required engineering controls include:

  • Sharps disposal containers that are puncture-resistant, leak-proof, and labeled with a biohazard symbol
  • Needleless systems and shielded needle devices where clinically appropriate
  • Self-sheathing needles and blunt-tip suture needles where appropriate
  • Plastic capillary tubes in place of glass where feasible

Engineering controls must be examined and maintained on a regular schedule. Sharps containers must be replaced when they reach the fill line, not when they are overflowing. Overfilled containers are a common source of needlestick injuries and a frequent basis for OSHA citations.

The Needlestick Safety and Prevention Act (NSPA), signed into law in 2000, strengthened this requirement by directing employers to evaluate and implement commercially available safety-engineered devices and to document that review annually.

  • Checklist item: Your facility uses safety-engineered sharps devices where available, maintains accessible sharps disposal containers, replaces them on a documented schedule, and records its annual safety-device evaluation.

Step 5: Enforce Work Practice Controls

  • Regulatory citation: 29 CFR 1910.1030(d)(2)(i)

Work practice controls reduce exposure risk by changing how tasks are performed. Unlike engineering controls, these depend on employee behavior. They must be written into policy and reinforced through training. Required work practice controls include:

  • No two-handed recapping of contaminated needles
  • No bending, shearing, or breaking of contaminated sharps
  • Immediate disposal of used sharps into a puncture-resistant container at the point of use
  • Handwashing with soap and water after removing gloves or after any skin contact with blood or OPIM
  • No eating, drinking, smoking, or applying cosmetics in areas where exposure is reasonably likely
  • No handling of contact lenses in exposure-risk areas
  • Minimal mouth pipetting

Work practice controls are not optional supplements to engineering controls. OSHA requires both, and inspectors evaluate whether work practice policies are documented, enforced, and reflected in employee training.

  • Checklist item: Your written ECP includes specific work practice controls for each high-risk task, and your training materials explain why each control is required.

Step 6: Provide Personal Protective Equipment at No Cost

  • Regulatory citation: 29 CFR 1910.1030(d)(3)

Employers must provide appropriate personal protective equipment (PPE) to employees at no charge. PPE selection must match the type and degree of exposure expected. Required types include:

  • Gloves (required any time blood or OPIM contact is anticipated)
  • Gowns, aprons, or lab coats for procedures involving splashing or spraying
  • Face shields or masks with eye protection when splash or aerosol risk exists
  • Resuscitation bags or pocket masks for emergency resuscitation procedures

All PPE must be accessible in appropriate sizes. Gloves must be replaced when torn or contaminated and may not be washed for reuse. Employees who are allergic to standard gloves must be provided alternatives at employer expense.

OSHA also requires that PPE be removed before leaving the work area and disposed of in designated containers. Employers must provide accessible containers for contaminated PPE and ensure that damaged PPE does not leave the facility without proper containment.

  • Checklist item: PPE is available in all required types and sizes, is provided at no cost, is located where employees need it, and your written policy covers removal and disposal procedures.

Step 7: Manage Regulated Waste and Maintain Housekeeping Standards

  • Regulatory citation: 29 CFR 1910.1030(d)(4) and 1910.1030(d)(2)(xiii)

Regulated waste under the BBP standard includes liquid or semi-liquid blood or OPIM, items saturated with blood or OPIM, sharps contaminated with blood or OPIM, and pathological waste. All regulated waste must be:

  • Placed in closable, puncture-resistant, and leak-proof containers
  • Labeled with the biohazard symbol in fluorescent orange or orange-red
  • Incinerated or decontaminated by a method (such as autoclaving) that effectively destroys bloodborne pathogens before disposal

Work surfaces, equipment, and bins that contact blood or OPIM must be cleaned and decontaminated with an EPA-registered disinfectant on a regular, documented schedule. Broken glassware must be handled with mechanical means, not by hand, even when wearing gloves.

Contaminated laundry must be bagged at the location of use and transported in labeled, leak-proof containers. Employees who handle contaminated laundry must wear gloves and other appropriate PPE.

  • Checklist item: Your facility has a documented housekeeping schedule, uses labeled regulated waste containers, and verifies that all waste is decontaminated or disposed of through a licensed regulated waste provider.

Step 8: Offer the Hepatitis B Vaccination Series

  • Regulatory citation: 29 CFR 1910.1030(f)(1) and (f)(2)

Employers must make the hepatitis B vaccination series available to every employee with occupational exposure. The offer must be made within 10 calendar days of initial assignment and at no cost to the employee. The vaccination must be provided by or under the supervision of a licensed healthcare professional.

Employees who choose to decline must sign a specific OSHA-prescribed declination form acknowledging the risk. That signed form must be kept in the employee's medical record and reviewed annually. If the employee later decides to accept vaccination, the employer must make it available.

Employers may not require a pre-vaccination antibody titer test as a condition of offering the vaccine, nor may they require employees to participate in a prescreening program. Post-vaccination titer testing is recommended by the U.S. Public Health Service guidelines but is not mandated by OSHA.

  • Checklist item: Your records show a hepatitis B vaccination offer to every exposed employee within 10 days of hire, signed declination forms where applicable, and current vaccination records kept confidentially in employee medical files.

Step 9: Establish Post-Exposure Evaluation and Follow-Up

  • Regulatory citation: 29 CFR 1910.1030(f)(3)

When an exposure incident occurs, the employer must make available a confidential medical evaluation and follow-up at no cost to the exposed employee. The required steps are:

  • Document the route of exposure and the circumstances under which it occurred
  • Identify and test the source individual for HBV and HIV infectivity, if feasible and if consent is given or not required by law
  • Collect and test the exposed employee's blood if the employee consents
  • Provide post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) where medically indicated
  • Provide counseling and evaluation of reported illnesses

The healthcare professional who performs the evaluation must provide a written opinion to the employer within 15 days of completing the evaluation. That opinion must state only whether the employee received the hepatitis B vaccine and whether vaccination is recommended. All other medical findings remain confidential.

Employees must be told the results of the source individual's testing and informed of post-exposure laws that apply.

  • Checklist item: Your facility has a written post-exposure response protocol posted visibly in clinical areas, and your staff know exactly who to report to, where to go for evaluation, and what forms to complete within the first hour after an incident.

Step 10: Train Employees and Maintain Required Records

  • Regulatory citation: 29 CFR 1910.1030(g) and 1910.1030(h)**

This step is the second most-cited area in OSHA BBP enforcement. The standard requires two categories of records: training records and medical records.

Training requirements:

Employees must receive bloodborne pathogens training at no cost, during working hours, at the time of initial assignment and at least annually after that. Training must be interactive and conducted by a person knowledgeable in the subject matter. The training session must cover:

  • The content of 29 CFR 1910.1030 and how to access it
  • The epidemiology and symptoms of bloodborne diseases
  • Transmission routes
  • The employer's exposure control plan and how to obtain a copy
  • Engineering and work practice controls in use at the facility
  • How to select, use, remove, handle, decontaminate, and dispose of PPE
  • The hepatitis B vaccination program and how to enroll
  • Post-exposure evaluation and follow-up procedures
  • Labels, signs, and color-coding used in the facility
  • How to report an exposure incident

Training records must include the date of each session, the content or a summary, the names and qualifications of the trainer, and the names and job titles of attendees. These records must be kept for three years from the training date.

Medical records:

Employee medical records related to BBP exposure, including vaccination status, declination forms, post-exposure evaluation results, and healthcare professional written opinions, must be maintained for the duration of employment plus 30 years, per 29 CFR 1910.1020.

Sharps injury log:

Employers required to maintain OSHA 300 injury and illness logs must also maintain a separate sharps injury log. Each entry must include the type and brand of device, the department or work area, and an explanation of how the incident occurred. The log must be maintained in a way that protects employee confidentiality.

Labels and signs:

Containers of regulated waste, refrigerators and freezers with blood or OPIM, and contaminated equipment must be labeled with the fluorescent orange or orange-red biohazard symbol. HIV and HBV research laboratory entrances must display warning signs. All labeling must be visible and attached before the container leaves the work area.

  • Checklist item: Your OSHA training records show completion dates, trainer qualifications, and attendee lists for initial and annual BBP sessions. Your facility maintains a separate sharps injury log, and all biohazard labels and signs are current and correctly posted.

The Most-Cited BBP Violations and How to Avoid Them

OSHA's enforcement record from 2013 through August 2025 shows the same violations appearing at facilities year after year. Knowing where inspectors focus helps you prioritize your internal audits:

  • No written Exposure Control Plan (1910.1030(c)(1)(i)) - the most-cited violation by frequency
  • Failure to provide employee training (1910.1030(g)(2)(i))
  • HBV vaccination not made available (1910.1030(f)(1)(i))
  • ECP not reviewed and updated annually (1910.1030(c)(1)(iv))
  • HBV vaccination not offered within 10 days of hire (1910.1030(f)(2)(i))
  • Missing or inadequate engineering controls (1910.1030(d)(2)(i))
  • No signed HBV declination form (1910.1030(f)(2)(iv))
  • Failure to document input from non-managerial employees on device selection (1910.1030(c)(1)(v))

None of these violations require special equipment or significant expense to correct. They are documentation and process failures. An internal compliance audit conducted before an inspection will find and fix most of them.
