Connecticut Medical Waste Disposal for Healthcare Facilities

Connecticut healthcare facilities must follow DEEP biomedical waste regulations for packaging, transport, treatment, and documentation. Get a free audit.

Back to Insights

Connecticut healthcare facilities that generate regulated waste must follow a specific set of state and federal requirements covering waste segregation, packaging, labeling, storage, transport, treatment, and documentation. The rules differ by waste type, and each category carries its own handling, disposal, and recordkeeping obligations.

Connecticut medical waste disposal is governed primarily by the Department of Energy and Environmental Protection (DEEP) under Section 22a-209-15 of the Regulations of Connecticut State Agencies (RCSA) and Connecticut General Statutes (CGS) Sections 22a-209b and 22a-209c. Federal requirements from OSHA, the EPA, and the DOT apply on top of state rules depending on the waste stream and handling activity.

A compliant program must address all of them, and this guide breaks down each requirement by waste type and facility obligation.

What Connecticut Defines as Biomedical Waste

Under Connecticut law, biomedical waste (BMW) means any infectious, pathological, or chemotherapy waste generated during the administration of medical care or during medical research involving humans or animals. The definition in CGS Section 22a-209b deliberately excludes hazardous waste and radioactive waste, both of which are regulated under separate legal frameworks.

Regulated medical waste in Connecticut covers a broad range of materials:

  • Sharps: hypodermic needles, syringes with or without attached needles, dental carpels, lancets, and scalpel blades
  • Infectious waste: body fluids, items saturated or dripping with blood, and waste generated from research involving infectious agents
  • Pathological waste: human tissue, organs, body parts, and discarded animal carcasses known to have been exposed to an infectious agent
  • Chemotherapy waste: antineoplastic agents and materials contaminated during chemotherapy administration

Note that biomedical waste generated by individuals at home is specifically excluded from BMW regulation. Waste generated in a patient's home by a visiting healthcare provider, however, is regulated as BMW.

  • Connecticut Medical Waste Regulations: DEEP and RCSA

DEEP administers Connecticut's biomedical waste program through RCSA Section 22a-209-15, which covers generator obligations, packaging standards, labeling, storage, transport, approved treatment methods, tracking, and recordkeeping. CGS Section 22a-209c establishes which disposal methods are legally permitted for each waste type.

Violations carry significant civil penalties. Under CGS Section 22a-226c, any person who disposes of biomedical waste in violation of state law can be assessed a civil penalty of up to $25,000 per offense. Each day of a continuing violation is treated as a separate offense.

Infectious Waste Disposal Methods

Infectious waste has three approved disposal pathways under Connecticut regulations. Incineration is always permitted. Steam sterilization (autoclaving) or alternative treatment technologies are permitted if approved in writing by DEEP.

Discharge to a sanitary sewer is also permitted, but only when the waste is in liquid or semi-solid form, secondary sewage treatment is available, no municipal ordinance prohibits the discharge, and all required permits have been obtained. Infectious waste treated and rendered unrecognizable on-site may then be disposed of as municipal solid waste.

Pathological and Chemotherapy Waste Disposal

Both pathological waste and chemotherapy waste must be disposed of by incineration. No alternative methods are permitted for these categories. Sewer discharge of pathological or chemotherapy waste is explicitly prohibited under Connecticut law, regardless of form or quantity.

This applies to human tissue, organs, body parts, antineoplastic drugs, and materials contaminated during chemotherapy treatment.

Sharps Disposal in Connecticut

Connecticut's definition of sharps under CGS Section 22a-209b(13) is broader than many facilities expect. It includes hypodermic needles, syringes with or without attached needles, indwelling catheters, introducers, dental carpels, lancets, and scalpel blades. Any item meeting this definition, regardless of size, must be placed in an approved sharps container before transport.

Sharps containers used for sharps disposal in Connecticut must be rigid, puncture-resistant, and leak-resistant. Each container must display the biohazard symbol or the word "BIOHAZARD" in clearly visible lettering. Containers cannot be reused unless their inner surfaces were protected from contamination during use or they have been thoroughly cleaned and disinfected with all visible traces of prior contamination removed.

Packaging, Labeling, and Biomedical Waste Storage Requirements

Every generator of biomedical waste in Connecticut must maintain a written Biomedical Waste Management Plan (BWMP) describing internal policies and procedures for handling all regulated waste streams. This plan is required from all generators, regardless of volume.

Before biomedical waste leaves a facility, generators must prepare it as follows:

  • Place waste in red bags to make it universally recognizable as BMW
  • Package the red bags in rigid, leak-proof, moisture-resistant outer containers
  • Use containers strong enough to resist tearing or bursting under normal handling conditions
  • Mark each container with the biohazard symbol or "BIOHAZARD" lettering
  • Seal all containers to prevent leakage in transit

Storage requirements under Connecticut regulations are equally specific. Biomedical waste must be stored separately from other waste materials. Access to storage areas must be limited to personnel authorized by the generator, transporter, or BMW treatment facility operator.

Outdoor storage areas, including dumpsters, sheds, and trailers, must remain locked. Storage area surfaces must be impermeable and easy to clean. Wherever BMW is stored, a sign displaying the biohazard symbol must be posted. Waste must be protected from water, precipitation, wind, and animals. Refrigeration during storage is permitted to prevent putrescence.

Moving Waste from Your Facility to Treatment

Connecticut follows a tracked chain of custody from waste generation through final treatment. The tracking requirements apply to generators, transporters, and treatment facilities.

Connecticut Biomedical Waste Tracking Form

Any generator who transports or offers BMW for transport must use the Connecticut Biomedical Waste Tracking Form found in Appendix 1 of RCSA Section 22a-209-15. The form must be prepared in enough copies to provide the generator, each transporter, and the BMW treatment facility with the required copies.

A transporter in Connecticut cannot legally accept biomedical waste without a completed tracking form accompanying the shipment.

Generators must retain copies of all tracking forms for a minimum of three years as part of their audit-ready waste documentation. This state tracking requirement functions separately from the federal Uniform Hazardous Waste Manifest (EPA Form 8700-22) used for RCRA hazardous waste shipments.

DEEP-Permitted Biomedical Waste Transporters

Any company in the business of transporting biomedical waste in Connecticut must hold a DEEP BMW Transporter Permit issued under RCSA Section 22a-209-15(g). Biomedical waste transporter permits must be renewed every two years.

A valid hazardous waste transporter permit does not authorize BMW transport. Connecticut requires separate permits for each waste category, and a transporter permitted to move RCRA hazardous waste is not automatically permitted to transport BMW.

Before signing a service contract, facilities should verify their vendor's current BMW permit status using DEEP's published list of permitted biomedical waste transporters.

DOT Medical Waste Transport Requirements

The U.S. Department of Transportation governs the transport of regulated medical waste under 49 CFR Parts 171 through 180. Under 49 CFR 173.197, regulated medical waste must be packaged in UN-standard, non-bulk rigid containers meeting Packing Group II performance standards. Sharps containers must be puncture-resistant and capable of retaining liquids. All packages must display biohazard markings.

Under DOT medical waste transport rules, the healthcare facility as shipper bears responsibility for proper packaging. All staff involved in packaging or signing shipping papers must complete DOT hazmat function-specific, safety, and awareness training. This training must be documented and available for inspection at the generating facility.

Pharmaceutical Waste Disposal and RCRA Hazardous Waste in Connecticut

Pharmaceutical waste is not the same as biomedical waste under Connecticut law, and the two cannot be managed identically. Certain pharmaceuticals are classified as RCRA hazardous waste, and their management follows the federal Resource Conservation and Recovery Act regulatory framework.

The EPA identifies hazardous waste pharmaceuticals under RCRA, including P-listed and U-listed drugs and pharmaceuticals exhibiting one or more hazardous characteristics: toxic, ignitable, corrosive, or reactive. Antineoplastic agents used in chemotherapy are commonly included.

Flushing hazardous waste pharmaceuticals down drains or disposing of them in regular trash is prohibited under both federal RCRA rules and Connecticut regulations.

Connecticut is currently in the process of adopting EPA's RCRA Subpart P regulations, which would establish healthcare-specific streamlined standards for managing hazardous waste pharmaceuticals. As of mid-2025, Connecticut has not yet completed that adoption.

Facilities managing hazardous waste pharmaceuticals should continue to comply with the current RCRA generator regulations while monitoring DEEP guidance on Subpart P's state-level effective date.

  • Hazardous Waste Generator Status: VSQG, SQG, and LQG

Every Connecticut facility generating RCRA hazardous waste must determine its generator category each calendar month. Connecticut still uses the term Conditionally Exempt Small Quantity Generator (CESQG) for the federal category that EPA now calls Very Small Quantity Generator (VSQG), but the thresholds mirror federal requirements.

The three categories are:

  • VSQG/CESQG: Generates 100 kg or less of non-acute hazardous waste per month
  • SQG: Generates more than 100 kg and less than 1,000 kg per month
  • LQG: Generates 1,000 kg or more per month, or more than 1 kg of acute hazardous waste per month

Generator status determines how long waste can accumulate on-site before shipment, what manifest and reporting requirements apply, and what training and emergency planning obligations exist.

Misclassifying generator status is one of the most common RCRA compliance errors in healthcare settings. A facility's status can change month to month based on actual waste output, and the correct category must be applied for each individual month.

e-Manifest Compliance and Recordkeeping

As of January 22, 2025, all SQGs and LQGs in Connecticut are required to register with the federal e-Manifest system for electronic tracking of hazardous waste shipments. VSQGs are not required to register. SQGs must also re-notify DEEP of their generator status every four years; the most recent deadline was September 1, 2025.

If a signed copy of the final hazardous waste manifest has not been submitted to the e-Manifest system within 60 days of the waste being accepted by the receiving facility, LQGs and SQGs must file an exception report with DEEP. Starting December 1, 2025, those exception reports must be submitted electronically through e-Manifest. Manifest copies must be retained for a minimum of three years.

Complete audit-ready waste documentation for RCRA hazardous waste includes manifests, waste determination records, training logs, storage inspection logs, and vendor verification.

  • OSHA Bloodborne Pathogens Standard: What Connecticut Facilities Must Do

Federal OSHA regulates occupational exposure to bloodborne pathogens under 29 CFR 1910.1030. Connecticut operates a state OSHA plan, but that plan covers only public sector workers. Private-sector healthcare facilities in Connecticut fall under federal OSHA jurisdiction and are subject to the full Bloodborne Pathogens Standard.

The standard applies to every employer with workers who have reasonably anticipated occupational contact with blood, body fluids, or other potentially infectious materials (OPIM). The requirements include:

  • A written exposure control plan that identifies all job roles with occupational exposure
  • Engineering controls, including sharps containers and needleless or needle-safe systems
  • An annual review and update of the exposure control plan, including documented consideration of safer medical devices with input from frontline clinical workers
  • A sharps injury log maintained by the employer
  • Hepatitis B vaccination offered to all employees with occupational exposure within 10 days of hire
  • Post-exposure evaluation and follow-up procedures
  • Bloodborne pathogens training for all exposed employees at no cost, during work hours, and repeated annually

Needlestick injury prevention is a direct statutory requirement following the 2000 Needlestick Safety and Prevention Act. Sharps containers are both a waste management requirement and a core employee safety control. Improper sharps disposal exposes not only clinical staff but also janitorial workers, waste handlers, and the public to needlestick injury risk.

Medical waste compliance training that covers bloodborne pathogens should be paired with your disposal program to keep staff current, documentation complete, and your facility ready for OSHA inspection. Who Needs Medical Waste Disposal Services in Connecticut

Connecticut law defines any person who owns or operates a facility that produces BMW in any quantity as a generator. There is no minimum volume threshold for that classification. When multiple generators occupy the same building, each separate business entity is treated as an independent generator for compliance purposes.

Connecticut medical waste disposal services cover facilities of every size and type. Any organization that produces biomedical waste in the state needs a compliant program. Regulated generators that require medical waste disposal services include:

  • Hospitals and multi-site health systems across Hartford, New Haven, Bridgeport, and the broader state
  • Private physician offices and family medicine practices
  • Dental offices, orthodontic practices, and oral surgery centers
  • Outpatient surgery and ambulatory care centers
  • Oncology and chemotherapy treatment facilities
  • Clinical, research, and university laboratories
  • Skilled nursing facilities and long-term care providers
  • Home health agencies whose nurses generate BMW at patient residences
  • Veterinary clinics and animal research facilities
  • Biotech and pharmaceutical research operations
  • Funeral homes and morgues
  • Tattoo studios and piercing shops that use regulated sharps
