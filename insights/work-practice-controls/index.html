Work Practice Controls: Definition, Examples, OSHA

Work practice controls definition, examples, and OSHA guidance for healthcare teams. Learn how these steps reduce exposure & support safer care daily.

Back to Insights Call 508-234-2400

Work practice controls are safety rules and procedures that reduce exposure risk by changing how a task is done. In healthcare and similar settings, they help lower the chance of contact with blood or other potentially infectious materials during everyday work.

A work practice control does not remove the hazard itself. Instead, it tells staff what steps to follow so the job is done in a safer way. That is why these controls are often paired with engineering controls, personal protective equipment, and training.

Under OSHA's Bloodborne Pathogens standard, employers must use engineering and work practice controls to eliminate or minimize employee exposure. The regulation defines work practice controls as controls that reduce the likelihood of exposure by altering how a task is performed. In practical terms, that means safer habits, safer sequencing, and safer handling rules built into daily care workflows.

Work practice controls definition

A simple work practice control definition is this: they are step-by-step job practices that lower exposure risk by changing worker behavior during a task. In healthcare, they are most often tied to bloodborne pathogens, sharps handling, hand hygiene, specimen handling, surface cleaning, and contaminated waste.

OSHA places them alongside engineering controls because safe equipment alone is not enough if staff still perform the task in an unsafe way.

When reviewing staff training, recordkeeping, and inspection preparation, compare the article guidance with OSHA and DOT training support before changing internal workflows.

What are work practice controls in a clinic or medical office? They are the written and taught procedures that staff follow every time a task involves possible exposure. Examples include washing hands after glove removal, never recapping contaminated needles with two hands, placing used sharps directly into approved containers, and keeping food and drinks out of exposure areas.

What best describes a work practice control

What best describes a work practice control is a behavior-based exposure prevention measure. It changes the manner of work instead of changing the device or environment. A self-sheathing needle is an engineering control. A rule that staff must activate the safety feature immediately after use is a work practice control. Both matter, but they solve the problem in different ways.

That distinction is important for compliance. If a facility buys safer sharps devices but staff do not follow the correct handling sequence, the exposure risk remains. Work practice controls turn policy into action. They are the bridge between equipment and day-to-day performance.

Work practice controls examples

Below are common work practice controls examples that apply in medical offices, outpatient clinics, dental settings, and many other healthcare environments:

Wash hands with soap and water as soon as feasible after glove removal or contact with blood or OPIM.

Do not bend, break, or recap contaminated needles by a two-handed technique.

Dispose of used sharps immediately in a closable, puncture-resistant, leak-proof sharps container.

Use techniques that minimize splashing, spraying, spattering, and droplet generation during procedures.

Keep eating, drinking, smoking, applying cosmetics, and handling contact lenses out of areas where exposure may occur.

Place specimens in proper containers before transport.

Decontaminate work surfaces after procedures, after spills, and at the end of the shift when contamination may have occurred.

Handle contaminated laundry as little as possible and bag it where it was used when required by the task and setting.

Follow post-exposure reporting steps immediately after a needle stick, sharps injury, or splash incident.

These examples answer several common searches at once: work practice controls include hand hygiene, sharps rules, cleaning steps, specimen handling, and other procedure-based protections.

An example of work practice control is the one-handed scoop method if recapping is unavoidable under a specific procedure, though the safer default is to avoid recapping altogether.

Work practice controls vs engineering controls

The main difference between work practice controls and engineering controls is where the protection happens.

Engineering controls act on the hazard itself. They isolate or remove the hazard from the workplace. Common examples include sharps disposal containers, self-sheathing needles, needleless systems, splash guards, and other safer medical devices.

Work practice controls act on the task. They reduce the chance of exposure by changing how the employee performs the job. Common examples include handwashing rules, no-food policies in exposure areas, immediate sharps disposal, and specific cleanup or reporting procedures.

Here is the simplest comparison:

  • Control Types and Their Functions
  • Engineering Controls

What it changes: The device, tool, or physical setup.

  • Example: Needleless IV system, sharps container.

Work Practice Controls

What it changes: The steps the worker follows.

  • Example: No two-handed recapping, hand hygiene after glove removal.

OSHA gives strong weight to engineering controls, but the standard requires both. In real healthcare settings, the best results come from combining safer devices with clear procedures, training, supervision, and follow-through.

Why work practice controls matter in healthcare

Healthcare workers face routine exposure risks from sharps injuries, blood contact, contaminated surfaces, and splash incidents. CDC notes that healthcare workers remain at risk for bloodborne pathogen exposure, including through needlesticks and other sharps injuries.

CDC also points facilities to sharps safety programs because these injuries remain a persistent problem in hospitals, clinics, nursing homes, and other care settings.

That is why work practice controls are not optional housekeeping details. They directly support safer patient care, lower injury risk, and stronger OSHA compliance. They also help standardize care across shifts and staff roles. When the correct sequence is written, trained, observed, and repeated, the job becomes safer and more consistent.

For readers who are building or updating OSHA programs, this topic ties directly to exposure control plans and the daily procedures staff follow after training.

Work practice controls for bloodborne pathogens compliance

In OSHA bloodborne pathogens compliance, work practice controls usually appear in the written exposure control plan and related procedures. A medical office manager should be able to answer four questions clearly:

What exact work practice controls apply to each task?

How are employees trained and refreshed on those controls?

How is compliance observed, corrected, and documented?

This is also where terms like other potentially infectious materials matter. Staff need to understand not only blood exposure, but also which materials and fluids fall within the exposure framework for their setting.

A strong program does more than list rules. It connects each rule to the task, the device, the cleanup step, the reporting step, and the person responsible. That makes training more useful and inspections less painful.

Examples by task in a medical office

A good way to explain what work practice controls is to connect them to specific tasks.

Blood draw or injection

Prepare the sharps container before starting.

Keep the patient area arranged to avoid awkward passing or reaching.

Activate the device safety feature as intended.

Dispose of the sharp immediately after use.

Room turnover and surface cleaning

Put on task-appropriate PPE before handling contaminated surfaces.

Use the approved disinfectant and follow the contact time.

Clean visible contamination as soon as feasible.

Replace protective coverings when contaminated or at the end of the shift if needed.

Remove PPE correctly and complete hand hygiene.

Specimen handling

Avoid carrying open or poorly secured specimens.

Move specimens in a way that limits leaks and splashes.

Clean and report any spill using the office procedure.

Laundry or waste handling

Handle contaminated textiles as little as possible.

Use the correct bagging and container process.

Do not compress or reach into waste containers by hand.

Escalate overfilled sharps containers immediately.

Common mistakes that weaken work practice controls

Many facilities know the term but still miss the execution. Common gaps include:

  • Policies that use general language but do not describe the actual task sequence
  • Training that covers rules once but does not verify competency
  • Safer devices in the office without matching procedure updates
  • Overfilled sharps containers or poor container placement
  • Hand hygiene expectations that are written but not reinforced
  • Cleanup steps that vary by employee or shift
  • Delayed incident reporting after splashes or sharps injuries

These gaps can show up during an inspection or after an injury. OSHA's bloodborne pathogens enforcement materials show that written exposure control plans, annual reviews, training, vaccination access, and engineering and work practice controls remain among the most cited areas.

If your team needs help turning written requirements into repeatable staff behaviors, focused OSHA training can close the gap between policy and practice.

How to improve work practice controls in your facility

The most effective way to improve work practice controls is to review them task by task.

Start by mapping exposure-prone procedures such as injections, blood draws, specimen handling, instrument cleanup, spill response, and waste handling. Then write the safest sequence for each task in plain language. Train staff on those exact steps.

Observe performance. Correct drift early. Update the procedure when the device, layout, staffing, or exposure risk changes.

A practical review checklist should include:

  • Current tasks with exposure potential
  • Required engineering controls for each task
  • Required work practice controls for each task
  • PPE expectations
  • Hand hygiene steps
  • Sharps disposal steps
  • Surface decontamination steps
  • Spill response steps
  • Post-exposure reporting steps
  • Training and retraining dates
  • Supervisor observation and correction process

Are work practice controls required by OSHA?

Yes. OSHA's Bloodborne Pathogens standard requires engineering and work practice controls to eliminate or minimize employee exposure when occupational exposure to blood or OPIM is present.

Work practice controls are one of the clearest examples of how compliance and daily safety meet. They tell staff exactly how to perform a task with less exposure risk, and they work best when paired with engineering controls, training, and regular review.
